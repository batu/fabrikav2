The 87-hour conductor session

One Codex TUI session, alive since Thu Jul 9, 22:08 — still running as of mining (Mon 13:41). It is not one agent: it is a parent router plus 115 named sub-agent forks (Arendt, Fermat, Turing, Dewey…) that together consumed 2.80B tokens.

Verdict: the parent thread is a pure router — 2.05B tokens in, 2.7M out, a 760:1 ratio — and 84% of its 11,315 tool calls are coordination. The product work happens in the forks and the spawned workers. What makes this session expensive is not laziness but two structural choices: it never resets (87 hours of accumulating history re-submitted at an average of 176k tokens per request, ~12k times), and every fork inherits the full parent history before doing its 5-minute job.

It is also genuinely impressive: it ran 10 consecutive unattended hours on Sunday at ~100% duty cycle, staged a six-fork document review swarm, ran the adversarial audit that caught four false-passes, and prepared a "morning rehearsal packet" for your return. The autonomy is warranted; the context economics are not.

What a fork costs before it starts

Every fork inherits the parent's full conversation history. By Sunday evening that means a fork opens at ~1.8B-counter depth with ~200k+ of context on its very first request — for a job that often lasts 5 minutes and emits 30k tokens. The six-fork U6 doc swarm re-submitted that inheritance six times over, in parallel.

Side effect on disk: each fork rollout replays the parent history — 17–18MB per fork by Sunday, ~700MB of the 719MB conductor footprint is replay copies.

Recommendations · specific to this session's shape

  1. Give it a bedtime. The session already writes "morning rehearsal packets" — make that the boundary: every night, emit the packet, kill the session, start fresh from the packet at wake. Context drops from ~200k+ back to ~15k, and nothing of value is lost — the packet is the value.
    At Sunday's rate (~280k avg context), a daily reset is worth hundreds of millions of input tokens per week.
  2. Fork from a brief, not from the bloodline. Review-swarm forks don't need 87 hours of history to check a doc's security section — spawn them as fresh codex exec with a 2-page brief. Reserve history-inheriting forks for work that genuinely needs the full conversation (rare).
    The U6 swarm alone re-submitted the inherited context ×6 in parallel; briefs would cut fork spend (0.78B) by an order of magnitude.
  3. Kill the abort-respawn storms, keep the reviewer diversity. (Revised 2026-07-13 after the rejection tribunal: the six-lens U6 swarm produced 17 real, traceably applied corrections — reviewer count earned its keep. The waste was elsewhere: the WXWTI85W morning fan-out respawned ~10 parallel reviewers into quota errors, timeouts, and routing mismatches — 6 of its 11 empty rounds were infra aborts, not opinions.) Check quota before fanning out; retry aborted reviewers serially, not in parallel; and record aborts as outcome_reason=abort, never as verdicts.
    Cuts the non-event rounds (30% of all recorded fails) without losing a single catch.
  4. Add a quota guard. It pegged the shared Codex rate window (≥95%) twice — starving the spawned workers that share the account. Before launching a swarm, check used_percent; defer bulk reviews above ~70%.
    Prevents the Thu-night and Sat-afternoon stalls from recurring.
  5. Move ledger/comment writing out of band. 318 telemetry/commentary calls composed in 200k-token context. A deterministic post-step (or a haiku-class scribe) can write ledger entries from structured results.
    Small tokens, but removes ~3% of calls and their results from premium history.

These slot into recommendations #1, #2 and #6 of the main report; this session is where most of that leverage physically lives.
